Platooning in Theory

February 18, 2026

Platooning in delivery is where multiple long-haul vehicles form a convoy as to avoid air resistance, promote vehicle longevity, and compartamentalize storage. Control theory research often uses platooning as a benefit of contributing more to robust multi-agent systems. However, with the marginal benefit and large organizational overhead, it does not seem very physically plausible.

We can model a multi-agent system with the following control law:

$$ x_i[k+1] = x_i[k] + (\sum_{j \sim i}{e_{ij}[k]}) + u[k]$$

Where \( x_i[k] \) is the state of agent \(x_i\) @ time \(k\).

Where \( e_{ij}[k] \) is the error between neighbors \( i \sim j \) @ time \(k\).

Where \( u[k] \) is a linear control operator @ time \(k\) (if the convoy travels at 10 m/s @ time \(k=[0,10]\), \(u[k] = 10)\).

In convoy-style platooning, our error can be defined as the following:

$$e_{i,i-1}[k] = (x_{i-1}[k] - x_i[k]) - d$$ or $$ e_{ij}[k] \;=\; \begin{cases} \big(x_{i-1}[k] - x_i[k]\big) - d, & \text{if } j = i-1,\\[4pt] 0, & \text{if} j \not = i -1 \text{.} \end{cases} $$

Where \(d\) is the expected distance between vehicles' CoMs

As for simplification, I'll primarily focus on air resistance--although there are other factors.

$$F_{net (u_i[k])} = m_{u_i[k]}a_{u_i[k]}$$

This will be modeled top down where the agents can be considered long cylinders.

So,

$$C=0.82$$

Using the standard quadratic drag model,

$$ F_{\text{air}(u_i[k])} = \frac{1}{2} C \rho A \big(u_i[k]\big)^2 $$

Where:

\( C = 0.82 \) (drag coefficient for a long cylinder)

\( \rho \approx 1.225 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \) (air density at sea level)

\( A \) is the frontal cross-sectional area

\( u_i[k] \) is the velocity of vehicle \( i \) at time \( k \)

Therefore, for the lead vehicle \( \mathbf{1} \),

$$ F_{\text{air}(u_{\mathbf{1}}[k])} = \frac{1}{2} (0.82) (1.225) A \big(u_{\mathbf{1}}[k]\big)^2 $$

However, in platooning, trailing vehicles experience reduced effective drag. This can be modeled by introducing a reduction factor \( \alpha_i \in (0,1] \), where

$$ F_{\text{air}(u_i[k])} = \frac{1}{2} C \rho A \alpha_i \big(u_i[k]\big)^2 $$

with

$$ \alpha_1 = 1, \quad \alpha_i < 1 \text{ for } i > 1. $$

The reduction factor \( \alpha_i \) is implicitly dependent on spacing \( d \). Smaller \( d \) induces stronger aerodynamic shielding, reducing drag and therefore lowering required control input magnitude.

The net longitudinal dynamics of each vehicle then become:

$$ m_i a_i[k] = u_i[k] - \frac{1}{2} C \rho A \alpha_i \big(u_i[k]\big)^2 $$

Although, the system is heavily simplified, this is how we could model such a system.

Drag grows quadratically with speed.

$$ F_{\text{drag}} = \frac{1}{2} C \rho A v^2 $$

Stopping distance also grows quadratically:

$$ d_{\text{stop}} = \frac{v^2}{2a} $$

So both benefit and risk scale with \( v^2 \).

Assume:

  • \( A = 10 \, m^2 \)
  • Max braking deceleration \( a = 5 \, m/s^2 \)
  • Approximate platoon reduction: 10% (I'll explain here)

Compare speeds:

Speed (m/s) Drag (N) 10% Savings (N) Stopping Distance (m)
15 1130 113 22.5
25 3139 314 62.5
35 6155 616 122.5
45 10178 1018 202.5

At low speed, platooning barely matters.

At highway speed (~25 m/s), savings become meaningful.

At high speed (35+ m/s), savings grow — but stopping distance doubles.

Beyond a certain regime, safe spacing must increase. That reduces aerodynamic shielding. Efficiency gains flatten.

Cool, but why are drag savings hovering 10%?

Even if individual vehicles see 15–17% drag reduction, the total convoy rarely exceeds ~10–13%.

Assume realistic reduction factors:

$$ \alpha = [1.00, 0.90, 0.85, 0.83] $$
Vehicle Reduction Drag (N)
1 (Lead) 0% 3139
2 10% 2825
3 15% 2668
4 17% 2605

Independent trucks:

$$ 4 \times 3139 = 12{,}556 \text{ N} $$

Platoon:

$$ 3139 + 2825 + 2668 + 2605 = 11{,}237 \text{ N} $$

Total reduction:

$$ \frac{12{,}556 - 11{,}237}{12{,}556} \approx 10.5\% $$

Even though some trucks see ~15–17% reduction, the convoy average compresses to ~10%.

Add more trucks:

$$ \alpha = [1.00, 0.90, 0.85, 0.83, 0.82, 0.82] $$

Total savings rises only to ~13%.

The lead vehicle anchors the average. Diminishing returns cap improvement.

So platooning is effective, but not dramatic.

Under realistic physics, total drag reduction clusters between:

$$ 8\% \; \text{to} \; 15\%. $$

With the organizational overhead, platooning does not seem effective (and safe) with current breaking technology.
