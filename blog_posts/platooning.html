Platooning in Theory

February 18, 2026

Platooning in delivery is where multiple long-haul vehicles form a convoy as to avoid air resistance, promote vehicle longevity, and streamline logistics. Control theory research often uses platooning as a benchmark for contributing to robust multi-agent systems. However, with the marginal benefit and large organizational overhead, it does not seem very physically plausible.

We can model a multi-agent system with the following control law:

$$ x_i[k+1] = x_i[k] + \left(\sum_{j \sim i}{e_{ij}[k]}\right) + f[k]$$

Where \( x_i[k] \) is the position of agent \(i\) at time \(k\).

Where \( e_{ij}[k] \) is the error between neighbors \( i \sim j \) at time \(k\).

Where \( f[k] \) is the nominal displacement input at time \(k\) (if the convoy moves at \(10\,\text{m/s}\) over a \(1\,\text{s}\) timestep, \(f[k] = 10\,\text{m}\)).

In convoy-style platooning, our error can be defined as the following:

$$e_{i,i-1}[k] = (x_{i-1}[k] - x_i[k]) - d$$ or $$ e_{ij}[k] \;=\; \begin{cases} \big(x_{i-1}[k] - x_i[k]\big) - d, & \text{if } j = i-1,\\[4pt] 0, & \text{if} j \not = i -1 \text{.} \end{cases} $$

Where \(d\) is the desired spacing between vehicles' centers of mass.

As for simplification, I'll primarily focus on air resistance--although there are other factors.

$$F_{\text{net},i}[k] = m_i \, a_i[k]$$

This will be modeled top down where the agents can be considered long cylinders.

So,

$$C=0.82$$

Using the standard quadratic drag model,

$$ F_{\text{air},i}[k] = \frac{1}{2} C \rho A \big(v_i[k]\big)^2 $$

Where:

\( C = 0.82 \) (drag coefficient for a long cylinder)

\( \rho \approx 1.225 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \) (air density at sea level)

\( A \) is the frontal cross-sectional area

\( v_i[k] \) is the velocity of vehicle \( i \) at time \( k \)

Therefore, for the lead vehicle \( \mathbf{1} \),

$$ F_{\text{air},\mathbf{1}}[k] = \frac{1}{2} (0.82) (1.225) A \big(v_{\mathbf{1}}[k]\big)^2 $$

However, in platooning, trailing vehicles experience reduced effective drag. This can be modeled by introducing a reduction factor \( \alpha_i \in (0,1] \), where

$$ F_{\text{air},i}[k] = \frac{1}{2} C \rho A \alpha_i \big(v_i[k]\big)^2 $$

with

$$ \alpha_1 = 1, \quad \alpha_i < 1 \text{ for } i > 1. $$

Smaller \( d \) induces stronger aerodynamic shielding, reducing drag and therefore lowering required thrust. The explicit dependence of \( \alpha_i \) on \( d \) is the whole story.

The net longitudinal dynamics of each vehicle then become:

$$ m_i \, a_i[k] = T_i[k] - \frac{1}{2} C \rho A \alpha_i \big(v_i[k]\big)^2 $$

Where \( T_i[k] \) is the propulsive thrust (force) applied by vehicle \( i \) at time \( k \).

Although the system is heavily simplified, this is how we could model such a system.

The key thing: \( \alpha_i \) is not a fixed constant; it depends on the gap \( d \) between vehicles. Wind tunnel data for trucks fits an exponential wake decay model:

$$ \alpha(d) = 1 - R_0 \, e^{-d / L} $$

where \( R_0 \approx 0.20 \) is the maximum possible drag reduction and \( L \approx 16.8 \, \text{m} \) is the aerodynamic wake decay length, calibrated against experimental data: ~11% reduction at \( d = 10 \, \text{m} \), ~4.5% at \( d = 25 \, \text{m} \).

Safe following distance isn't a free parameter either. It's bounded below by kinematics:

$$ d_{\text{safe}}(v) = \frac{v^2}{2a} + 0.5 \, v $$

where 0.5 s is the assumed V2V communication latency. Drag also grows quadratically:

$$ F_{\text{drag}} = \frac{1}{2} C \rho A v^2 $$

So both effects scale with \( v^2 \), which means stopping distance grows at the same rate as drag. The question is which grows faster relative to the wake length \( L \).

The crossover happens where stopping distance equals the wake's useful range. The wake carries meaningful shielding out to roughly \( d^* \approx 23 \, \text{m} \) (where reduction falls to 5% of its maximum). Setting \( d_{\text{stop}} = d^* \):

$$ \frac{v^2}{2a} = d^* \implies v^* = \sqrt{2 \, a \, d^*} \approx \sqrt{2 \times 5 \times 23} \approx 15 \, \text{m/s} \approx 55 \, \text{km/h} $$

Above ~55 km/h, stopping distance exceeds the aerodynamic wake length. You are forced to space farther than the wake extends, and \( \alpha_i \to 1 \), so shielding collapses.

Assume \( A = 10 \, \text{m}^2 \), \( a = 5 \, \text{m/s}^2 \), and autonomous headway \( t_r = 0.5 \, \text{s} \) (representative of current V2V communication latency). 4-truck platoon:

Speed (m/s) Speed (km/h) Safe gap (m) Follower \(\alpha\) 4-truck savings
10 36 15.0 0.918 6.9%
15 54 30.0 0.967 2.8% <- crossover
20 72 50.0 0.990 0.9%
25 90 75.0 0.998 0.2%
35 126 140.0 1.000 ~0%
45 162 225.0 1.000 ~0%

This is the crux of it. As speed increases, drag grows with \( v^2 \), which naively makes platooning more appealing. But safe spacing also grows with \( v^2 \), pushing followers outside the wake faster. The two effects nearly cancel, and above ~55 km/h the spacing requirement wins.

Highway platooning at 90 km/h with autonomous latency gives under 1% total savings. That's essentially noise.

So where do the 10–15% figures in the literature come from?

Those numbers are measured at very tight gaps — 4 to 10 meters — which are only achievable with sub-100ms autonomous reaction times and assume no emergency braking scenario. At \( d = 10 \, \text{m} \) and \( v = 25 \, \text{m/s} \), stopping distance alone is 62.5 m. You'd rear-end the lead vehicle before your brakes fully engaged.

The 10% figure is physically real but the conditions that produce it are not operationally safe. At any gap that gives genuine safety margins, savings collapse well below that.

The lead vehicle anchors the average regardless; it sees zero benefit and carries full drag. Diminishing returns hit fast after vehicle 2. Under any realistic safe spacing, total convoy reduction sits closer to:

$$ 1\% \; \text{to} \; 7\% $$

depending on speed and headway regime.

With the organizational overhead, platooning does not seem effective (and safe) with current braking technology.
